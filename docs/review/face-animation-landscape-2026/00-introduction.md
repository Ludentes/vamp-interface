# Chapter 00 — Introduction: The Three Worlds and the Bridges Between Them

## The Fragmentation Problem

A face, as an object of computation, is simultaneously more studied and more fragmented than almost any other visual subject. Faces have been parameterized, tracked, rigged, fit, reconstructed, generated, animated, warped, driven, rendered, and embedded — each of these verbs supported by a community that has built its own tools, its own datasets, its own standards, and its own shared assumptions. A newcomer to the field in 2026 will quickly notice that these communities do not cite each other uniformly. An engineer deep in VTuber production can name a dozen Live2D tools and does not know what FLAME is. A PhD student working on 3D morphable models has read fifty papers that assume FLAME as the substrate and has never opened Cubism Editor. A diffusion researcher designing a new face-editing adapter references ControlNet, IP-Adapter, and InstantID as a matter of course and is probably unaware that MediaPipe ships ARKit-compatible blendshape scores out of the box.

This fragmentation is not an artifact of sloppy literature — it reflects genuinely different design goals, performance envelopes, and user populations. But when one actually tries to build something — a VTuber tool, a data visualization, a talking-head application, a synthetic dataset pipeline — the fragmentation becomes friction. Decisions that should be routine require cross-community archaeology. And decisions are routinely made against an incomplete map, which leads to the characteristic failure mode of this field: ambitious prototypes that pick the wrong substrate and cannot be extended.

This review is written against that failure mode. Its thesis is simple. The fragmentation is real but it is not mysterious. The landscape has structure. Once you see the structure, decisions become tractable.

## The Three Worlds

The structure, as we will develop it, has three primary worlds, and the rest of the landscape consists of the bridges between them.

**The first world is 2D rigged animation.** Its canonical tool is Live2D Cubism, its output format is `.moc3`, its driver applications are VTube Studio and VSeeFace, and its community is predominantly VTubers, indie creators, and the hololive-style talent agencies that built a multi-billion-dollar industry on top of it. The representation is fundamentally non-photographic: a face here is a layered sprite atlas controlled by fifty to a hundred named scalar parameters (`PARAM_EYE_L_OPEN`, `PARAM_MOUTH_SMILE`) that drive 2D mesh deformations. Identity is established by an artist's illustration; animation is established by an artist's rigging work. The computational layer is thin — most of the artistry is hand-authored — and the runtime cost is negligible. This world is aesthetically committed to stylization, specifically to the anime idiom, and it shows no particular interest in photorealism.

**The second world is 3D parametric.** Its canonical model is FLAME (Faces Learned with an Articulated Model and Expressions), published by MPI-IS in 2017, and its lineage traces back through Blanz and Vetter's 1999 Morphable Model. A face here is a 5,023-vertex mesh whose shape is parameterized by a ~300-component PCA basis, whose expression is parameterized by a ~100-component PCA basis, and whose pose is parameterized by jaw, neck, and eye rotations. The representation is explicit, orthogonal by construction, and serves as a shared substrate for an enormous research ecosystem — over two hundred downstream papers use FLAME as their underlying model. The aesthetic output of raw FLAME is unremarkable: a smooth gray mannequin, PS2-era geometric fidelity. Photorealism is achieved by layering a neural renderer on top, and this layering is where most of the recent innovation sits. This world's center of gravity is academic — it is where PhD theses get written, where CVPR and SIGGRAPH papers get published, and where the canonical representations have been stable for nearly a decade. The community is scholarly and the tooling reflects that: open-source, research-oriented, not always easy to run.

Parallel to FLAME in this world, but with a different center of gravity, sits a second tradition: the production animation standard built around **ARKit's 52 blendshapes**, introduced by Apple in 2017 and adopted, copied, or referenced by effectively every major real-time face pipeline since. This is the standard that MetaHuman expects, that VRoid and VRChat produce, that MediaPipe deliberately outputs in its blendshape head, and that every VTuber face-tracking stack speaks. ARKit blendshapes are hand-authored, semantic, and bounded in `[0, 1]`. They are not in general interconvertible with FLAME expression PCA components except through a learned least-squares solver, and the two representations have lived in parallel communities with only moderate cross-pollination. ARKit-flavored blendshapes are the *production* lingua franca; FLAME is the *research* lingua franca; the conversion between them is roughly one millisecond of compute and a small amount of cultural distance.

**The third world is neural implicit.** Here a face is not represented by explicit vertices or by named parameters at all — it is represented by the weights and activations of a neural network, or by a cloud of learned 3D Gaussians, or by the features of a diffusion model's latent space. This world emerged from the 2018-2021 explosion of NeRF, StyleGAN, and then Gaussian Splatting, and it produces the photorealistic outputs that the first two worlds cannot match. Within this world there are several sub-traditions that are developing at different rates: GAN-based portrait editing is mature but losing mindshare; NeRF-based avatars peaked around 2023 and have been largely displaced by 3D Gaussian Splatting; diffusion-based face editing is currently the most active research front; and a parallel lineage of implicit-keypoint neural deformation (LivePortrait, X-Portrait) is producing some of the most practical real-time tools without using any 3DMM at all.

These three worlds — 2D rigged, 3D parametric, neural implicit — are the scaffold of this review. But the interesting structure lives in the bridges.

## The Bridges

Between 2D rigged and 3D parametric, there is almost nothing. Live2D parameters and FLAME parameters are incommensurable: different dimensionalities, different semantics, different mathematical structure (hand-authored 2D scalars versus PCA over 3D vertex positions). Converting between them requires a learned mapping trained on paired data, and no such mapping has been published. In practice, the bridge is taken at a different place — both worlds accept ARKit-style blendshapes as a driving signal, and both worlds accept MediaPipe or ARKit tracking output at runtime. The bridge, in other words, is the blendshape standard. Live2D creators ingest tracking data via VTube Studio's OpenSeeFace or iFacialMocap. 3DMM researchers ingest tracking data via ARKit or MediaPipe and solve into FLAME parameters. Neither side knows or cares about the other's internal representation; they agree on the API boundary.

Between 3D parametric and neural implicit, the bridges are thick and active. RigFace, Arc2Face with its expression adapter, MorphFace, and HeadStudio all use FLAME parameters as conditioning signals for diffusion models or 3DGS avatars. GaussianAvatars, 3D Gaussian Blendshapes, and SPARK all attach neural rendering primitives to FLAME meshes and drive them by FLAME parameters. This is the most active research front in the field as of 2026. The open problem is not that the bridges don't exist but that they are not yet packaged as products. The ingredients to do "embedding or text → novel photorealistic 3D avatar animated by live face capture in real time" are all publicly available, but nobody has put them in one box.

Between 2D rigged and neural implicit, the bridge is LivePortrait and its heirs — a tradition that bypasses FLAME and 3DMMs entirely, learns its own implicit keypoint structure, and warps a source portrait to match a driving video's motion. LivePortrait runs in 12.8ms on a consumer GPU, accepts anime or photorealistic input, handles both styles competently, and has been adopted by ComfyUI plugins and independent tools. It makes no commitments about the internal structure of what a face is. It operates one level above that question. This is a philosophically important bridge because it points at the possibility that the whole 3DMM machinery may be unnecessary for a large subset of use cases.

## The Shape of This Review

Chapters 01 through 04 establish vocabulary and then tour the three worlds in turn, starting with a formal taxonomy and then walking through Live2D (Chapter 02), the MediaPipe/ARKit blendshape standard (Chapter 03), and FLAME and its research ecosystem (Chapter 04). Chapters 05 through 08 cover the neural implicit side: first the LivePortrait lineage (Chapter 05), then audio-driven talking heads (Chapter 06), then 3DGS avatars (Chapter 07), and finally diffusion-based parametric generation (Chapter 08). Chapter 09 explicitly catalogs the bridges between representations and what it costs to cross each one. Chapter 10 folds everything into decision trees organized by use case: if you are building X, use Y because of Z. Chapter 11 assesses the market, tooling reality, and community preference distribution — which tools people actually use, not just which tools they should use. Chapter 12 closes with conclusions, open problems, and a 12-to-24-month outlook.

The review is long. Readers looking for a specific answer should skip to Chapter 10 first; readers looking for a worldview should read linearly. Chapters 01 and 03 are foundational — subsequent chapters assume their vocabulary.

## A Note on What This Review Is Not

This review is not a tutorial. It does not walk through installing Cubism Editor, running EMOCA on a photo, or training a RigFace model. The original research notes in both project `research/` folders cover those ground-level specifics, and we reference them rather than reproduce them.

This review is not an exhaustive paper survey. Several hundred papers sit within scope, and citing all of them would produce a bibliography with no argument. We cite what is load-bearing for the structure and gesture at what isn't.

This review is not impartial about quality. Some widely-cited methods have been superseded; some obscure methods are technically important. We mark these judgments explicitly and give reasoning. The reader is free to disagree.

This review is written in April 2026. The field is moving fast enough that specific model names and performance numbers will age within six to twelve months. The structural claims — about the three worlds, about the bridges, about where the research front sits — should age more slowly. If this review is being consulted after 2027, treat specific tools and benchmarks as historical and treat the taxonomy as still approximately valid.
