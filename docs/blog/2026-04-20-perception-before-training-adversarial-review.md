# Adversarial Review — Perception Before Training

## Summary
- 3 flagged items total: 1 SOLID (I.3, with caveats), 1 SOFT (I.1), 1 OVERSTATED (I.2).
- Top 3 issues to fix before publishing:
  1. The TDD "validation" of the skill is self-marking homework — the baseline was run once, by the same author, with no blinding, no adjudication, no second baseline draw. It cannot support the weight the post puts on it.
  2. "FFA machinery / holistic / configural processing" is imported from human-face neuroscience and applied to Flux-generated synthetic faces without argument. The inversion-control licensing (Yin/Rossion) assumes the stimuli recruit FFA circuitry; this is precisely what has not been established.
  3. The "no Claude skill exists, therefore we wrote one" move confuses absence-of-skill with presence-of-need and frames a workflow-preference decision as a literature finding.
- **Overall recommendation: fix and republish.** The core methodological pivot (perception is prior) is defensible. The self-congratulatory skill-validation narrative and the unflagged neuroscience borrowings are not, and they are load-bearing.

## Pass 1 — Claim-by-claim

### Claim I.1 — "Perceptual-readability is logically prior to and separable from the training-pipeline question"
**Rating:** SOFT.
**Critique:** "Logically prior" and "separable" are doing different work and the post conflates them. *Prior* is defensible — if humans can't read faces, the downstream consumer fails. *Separable* is not. The training pipeline's four-term loss includes a human-perception-aligned term in all practical versions (or it's optimizing for an encoder proxy that I.1 itself argues is untrustworthy). If perception measurements feed back into loss weights or sampling policy, the two questions are coupled, not separable. The author's own §"What changed in our plan of record" concedes this: Stage 0 now takes perceptual D₇₉ as an input. That's coupling, not separation. Also: "you cannot use training-pipeline losses as a proxy" is asserted, not argued — it requires showing machine-metric/perceptual decorrelation, which is exactly what the curriculum hasn't yet measured.
**Recommendation:** Downgrade from "logically prior and separable" to "currently unmeasured and potentially decorrelated." Drop the separability claim or produce the decorrelation evidence.

### Assumption I.2 — "The skill investment pays off beyond this curriculum"
**Rating:** OVERSTATED (in the surrounding prose, not the box itself — the box is honest; the rest of the post treats it as resolved).
**Critique:** The box says "hoped, not proved," which is fine. But then the rest of the post — "methodology-gap problems can be closed durably, not ad hoc," "versioned infrastructure," "imported discipline" — speaks as if the payoff is established. One TDD pass on one scenario, same-author same-session, is not evidence of reusability; it's evidence that adding a checklist to a prompt causes the checklist items to appear in the output. That's tautological, not validating. Skills-rot, pivot-risk, and single-user risk are all real and not priced in. The decision may still be correct on expected value, but it's being sold as validated.
**Recommendation:** Either price in a concrete reuse forecast (what are the next 3 perception studies, and when?) or drop the "versioned infrastructure / durably-closed" framing and keep it as "bet we made, will revisit."

### Claim I.3 — "Face-specific claims require face-vs-comparator AND face-vs-inverted CIs"
**Rating:** SOLID — but narrower than presented.
**Critique:** The Rossion/Yin inversion logic is correctly summarized for *human* upright-face advantage as evidence of configural/holistic processing. However, the claim is imported into a setting where the stimuli are Flux-generated synthetic portraits optimized for photorealism under diffusion priors. Whether inversion-based FFA licensing transfers to synthetic faces at short SOAs with backward masks is itself a live empirical question — the face-inversion effect is known to attenuate for atypical, caricatured, or distribution-shifted faces, and the magnitude varies across paradigms. The CI-decomposition is methodologically sound; the strong reading ("licenses face-specific processing") is not automatic. Also missing: the composite-face effect (Young 1987 / Richler) is the more modern holistic-processing diagnostic; inversion alone is weaker evidence than the post implies.
**Recommendation:** Keep the two-CI structure. Soften "licenses face-specific processing" to "is a necessary (not sufficient) condition" and note the synthetic-stimuli transfer risk explicitly.

## Pass 2 — Unflagged assumptions

1. **"FFA machinery, holistic perception, configural processing" applies to Flux portraits.** This is the biggest unflagged import. These constructs are defined in human-face neuroscience on photographs of real faces (or tightly controlled line-drawings). Diffusion-generated portraits have known artifact signatures (symmetry violations, texture priors, hand/ear anomalies) that may either suppress or spuriously trigger FFA-like responses. The post uses these terms as if naming them summons them. They don't.

2. **"Two reasons we landed on a multi-level curriculum."** Presented as exhaustive. Obvious third reason: the author wanted the curriculum to justify the skill investment; a single decisive trial would not have. Obvious fourth: publication-unit hedging — six levels means any one positive result is shippable. Neither is named.

3. **"The four families" / "the 11-item checklist" / "six levels."** Taxonomic closure presented as discovered, not constructed. No argument that the list is MECE, that adjacent disciplines (HCI, visualization, signal-detection-in-radiology) were surveyed for alternative frame-of-reference paradigms, or that the levels aren't a linear narrative imposed on a branching design space.

4. **"Zero hits on perception, psychophysics, 2-AFC, d′, staircase" → "the skill didn't exist and we needed to write it."** Non-existence-in-Claude-skills-registries is a claim about the tooling ecosystem, not about the knowledge ecosystem. Kingdom & Prins exists. Palamedes exists. psignifit exists. PsychoPy exists. The relevant question isn't "is there a Claude skill" but "is there a reason the procedural knowledge needs to live in a Claude skill specifically rather than in a reference doc + reading list." That's a workflow-preference, not a gap in the world, and the post dresses it up as the latter.

5. **"The baseline was competent… what the baseline missed was exactly what the skill was for."** Circular. The skill was designed with knowledge of what a baseline subagent would miss (the author already knew the perception literature gaps at the point of designing the skill, else they couldn't have written it). The "test" therefore cannot discriminate between "skill adds value" and "skill is a checklist of things the author wanted in the output." A real TDD would have: a second independent author writing the scenario, blinded scoring rubric defined before either run, multiple baseline draws to estimate variance, a non-perception skill as a control (does *any* skill close the gap equivalently?). None of this was done.

6. **"Demographic-PC orthogonalization is a global invariant."** Stated as established. It's a plausible engineering hypothesis about Flux latent space that has not been measured for *this* pipeline, and the proposed orthogonalization (project out classifier-aligned directions) assumes linear decomposition of perceptual distance — which is precisely what the rest of the post says you can't assume.

7. **"The classical FFA literature trains its observers."** Some does; much doesn't. FFA localizers typically use passive viewing. Training is common in behavioral face-expertise paradigms (Gauthier greebles), less so in inversion-effect demonstrations. The sentence blurs "psychophysics sometimes trains observers" into "FFA literature trains observers" to license a training step the curriculum wants anyway.

8. **"Small-N = 3–5 is enough for existence/mechanism claims under Smith & Little."** Smith & Little (2018) is real and does license small-N for specific designs with dense per-subject data, but the licensing is conditional on within-subject replicability measurements and model-based inference. Citing the paper is not the same as meeting its conditions; the post cites it as a permission slip.

9. **"Preregistration is mandatory… doesn't prevent exploratory analysis."** True as stated but papered over: AsPredicted for a six-level curriculum where later levels depend on earlier-level parameters is not straightforward preregistration. Adaptive designs require different preregistration structures (sequential analysis, alpha-spending). Not mentioned.

10. **The rhetorical shift from "baseline missed inversion control" to "skill is validated."** This is the core motivated-reasoning move. The measured outcome is "adding X to the prompt causes X to appear in the output." That is trivially true of any prompt addition. The conclusion "skill is validated" does not follow.

## Pass 3 — Structural critique

The "interlude" framing is doing real work the post doesn't acknowledge. Interludes don't get full adversarial review, don't need exhaustive citations, and can make gestural arguments ("we had argued extensively from priors"). This gives the author license to ship a pivot and a tooling commitment in the same document while deferring the accountability for either. A stand-alone post titled "We built a perception-experiment-design skill and here's why it's validated" would be held to a higher bar; the interlude frame lets that claim ride on the back of the more defensible "perception is prior" pivot.

Motivated reasoning is most visible in the skill-validation section. The chain is: (a) we noticed the first-draft curriculum was weak, (b) we built a skill, (c) we tested it by comparing a baseline subagent to a skill-loaded subagent, (d) the skill-loaded output closed the gaps. Step (c) is where the argument breaks. The author wrote the skill knowing the gaps, then tested whether a prompt containing a list of gap-closing items closes the gaps. Of course it does. The test is not adversarial; it's confirmatory by construction. A genuine test would have asked: does the skill close gaps the author *didn't* already know about, on scenarios the skill wasn't designed against? That test wasn't run.

Citation selection is lopsided in the pro-small-N, pro-FFA, pro-inversion direction. Absent from the citation set: anything from the replication-crisis side of psychophysics (small-N designs are precisely what Simonsohn et al. have criticized in other forms); anything on the limits of face-inversion transfer to synthetic or atypical faces; anything from the data-visualization literature on why chart-vs-face comparisons are hard to specify fairly (the post admits "plural comparators" at D≥3 but doesn't cite the actual visualization-evaluation literature — Heer, Kay, Munzner — which has thought hard about this).

A hostile reviewer from **face perception / psychophysics** pushes back first on: synthetic-stimuli transfer of inversion licensing; adaptive vs fixed-grid preregistration mismatch; whether Smith & Little's small-N conditions are actually met; whether backward masking at 200/400ms SOAs is appropriate for these stimuli; the missing composite-face design. A hostile reviewer from **ML / generative-model evaluation** pushes back first on: why a psychophysics curriculum rather than a large-N crowdsourced design with standard IRT; why hand-designed D-dim vectors rather than sampling from the real task distribution (the ecological transfer is now gated behind a six-level curriculum that could run for a year); whether the whole thing is measuring Flux artifacts rather than pattern-recovery. A hostile reviewer from **Claude-skills / tooling** pushes back first on: the self-refereed TDD; the conflation of "skills-registry gap" with "knowledge gap"; the "skills rot" acknowledgment being cosmetic; and why the skill needs to exist as a skill rather than as a reference doc plus reading list.

The strongest part of the post is the candid §"What this interlude doesn't claim." The weakest is the skill-validation narrative masquerading as a TDD pass.

## Final verdict
The pivot is correct and the curriculum is plausibly well-designed; publish the pivot. The skill-validation story is self-refereed and should be rewritten as "bet made, rationale, explicit reuse forecast, revisit date" rather than as a TDD pass. The unflagged neuroscience imports (FFA, holistic, configural) need a Claim box of their own acknowledging that the licensing literature was developed on human photographic stimuli and its transfer to Flux synthetic portraits is itself a curriculum question, not a background assumption. Fix those three things and the post survives adversarial review; ship as-is and a competent psychophysics reviewer will dismantle the skill-validation section in a paragraph.
